**Name:** Pyridinium chlorochromate  
**Common abbreviation:** PCC  
**Type:** Oxidising agent  
**Common use:** Oxidation of alcohols under relatively mild, anhydrous conditions.

PCC is often taught as a way to oxidise primary alcohols to aldehydes without continuing to carboxylic acids under the usual controlled conditions.

## What it oxidises

PCC commonly oxidises:

- primary alcohols to aldehydes
- secondary alcohols to ketones

Tertiary alcohols normally do not oxidise under simple PCC conditions because they do not have a suitable hydrogen on the alcohol-bearing carbon.

## Why PCC matters

The key teaching point is selectivity.

Many strong aqueous oxidants can take primary alcohols all the way to carboxylic acids. PCC is used when the desired product is the aldehyde.

## Common exam traps

- Do not oxidise a primary alcohol to a carboxylic acid when PCC is specified.
- Do not oxidise tertiary alcohols without additional chemistry.
- Check whether the question uses aqueous strong oxidant conditions instead of PCC.
- PCC contains chromium, so real lab use requires waste and safety handling under local rules.

## Safety and handling context

Chromium-containing reagents require controlled handling and disposal.

For real laboratory work, follow the local risk assessment, supervisor instructions and current SDS. This note is for study and documentation, not a substitute for lab-specific safety procedures.
