Thin-layer chromatography, or **TLC**, is a quick way to check whether a reaction mixture still contains starting material, whether product is forming and whether obvious by-products are present.

## What TLC answers

TLC is useful for three routine questions:

- Has the starting material disappeared?
- Has a new product spot appeared?
- Is the reaction mixture getting cleaner or more complex?

It does not prove full structure by itself. It is a monitoring tool, not final characterisation.

## Basic workflow

1. Spot the starting material reference.
2. Spot the reaction mixture.
3. Co-spot starting material and reaction mixture if needed.
4. Develop the plate in an appropriate solvent system.
5. Visualise spots under UV or with a stain.
6. Compare spot positions and intensities.

## Rf value

The retention factor is:

**Rf = distance travelled by compound / distance travelled by solvent front**

Rf values are only comparable when the same stationary phase, solvent system and plate conditions are used.

## Choosing a solvent system

The aim is to separate spots clearly.

If all spots stay near the baseline, the solvent system is too non-polar for the compounds.

If all spots run with the solvent front, the solvent system is too polar.

For many undergraduate organic reactions, a hexane/ethyl acetate mixture is a common starting point.

## Common mistakes

- Forgetting to mark the solvent front before it evaporates.
- Spotting too much sample and causing streaking.
- Letting the solvent level sit above the baseline.
- Comparing Rf values from different solvent systems as if they are absolute.
- Assuming TLC alone proves product identity.

## Quick summary

TLC is a fast reaction monitoring method.

Use it to decide whether to continue, quench, purify or change conditions, then confirm product identity with stronger characterisation data.
